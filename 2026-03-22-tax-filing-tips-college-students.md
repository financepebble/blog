# Tax Filing Tips for College Students: Your 2026 Filing Season Survival Guide

*April 15, 2026 is the deadline. Here's what your college student — or you, on their behalf — needs to know right now.*

---

Let me be direct with you: most parents of college students, and most college students themselves, leave real money on the table every single tax season. Not because the rules are impossible — but because nobody sat them down and explained the game clearly.

That changes today. Whether you're a parent helping a 19-year-old navigate their first W-2, or a 23-year-old grad student figuring out whether your tuition waiver is taxable, this guide covers the **2025 tax year** (filed by **April 15, 2026**) with numbers verified directly from IRS official publications — including the updated thresholds introduced by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBB, P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025).

Let's walk through it together.

---

## Does Your College Student Even Need to File?

This is always the first question — and the answer depends on two things: income type and whether someone can claim the student as a dependent.

One important context note first: the OBBB raised the 2025 standard deduction for single filers to **$15,750**, which flows directly into the filing thresholds below.

### Filing Thresholds for Tax Year 2025

| Student Type | Condition | Must File If... |
|---|---|---|
| **Independent student** — single, not a dependent, under 65 | Gross income | Gross income ≥ **$15,750** |
| **Dependent student** — earned income only | Wages, tips, taxable scholarships | Earned income > **$15,750** |
| **Dependent student** — unearned income only | Dividends, interest, capital gains | Unearned income > **$1,350** |
| **Dependent student** — both types | Gross income | Greater of **$1,350**, or earned income (up to $15,300) **+ $450** |
| **Self-employed student** (any dependency status) | Net self-employment earnings | Net earnings ≥ **$400** |

*Source: [IRS Publication 501 (2025), Tables 1 & 2](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p501)*

> **Mentor tip:** Even if your student doesn't *have* to file, they almost certainly *should*. If their employer withheld federal income tax from any paycheck, filing is the only way to get it back. A refund sitting unclaimed is money left on the table.

## Are You (or Is Your Student) Someone's Dependent?

This is the question that drives almost everything else — because it determines who gets to claim the valuable education tax credits.

The IRS allows parents to claim a full-time student as a **Qualifying Child** dependent if all of the following apply:

- The student is **under age 24** at the end of the tax year
- They were a **full-time student for at least 5 months** during 2025
- They did **not provide more than half of their own financial support**
- They lived with the parent for more than half the year (college absences are generally excepted)

**Why does this matter so much?** Because if your parents claim you as a dependent, you generally cannot claim the American Opportunity Tax Credit or Lifetime Learning Credit on your own return — those credits belong to whoever claims the dependency. This is a coordination decision families should make *together, before anyone files.*

*Source: [IRS Publication 501 (2025), Qualifying Child Rules](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p501)*

## The Big Two: Education Tax Credits in 2025

> **Deep dive available:** We covered these credits in full detail — including phase-out math and real-world scenarios — in our earlier post: [Education Tax Credits 2025: AOTC vs. Lifetime Learning Credit Explained](https://financepebble.com/tax/education/personal-finance/2026/02/15/education-tax-credits-2025.html). The table below is your quick-reference summary.

### AOTC vs. Lifetime Learning Credit — Side-by-Side (2025)

| Feature | American Opportunity Credit (AOTC) | Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) |
|---|---|---|
| **Max credit** | **$2,500** per eligible student | **$2,000** per tax return |
| **Refundable?** | Yes — up to **$1,000** (40% refundable) | No — non-refundable only |
| **Years of eligibility** | First **4 years** of post-secondary only | **Unlimited** years |
| **Enrollment requirement** | At least **half-time** | At least **one course** |
| **MAGI phase-out begins** | $80,000 single / $160,000 MFJ | $80,000 single / $160,000 MFJ |
| **MAGI phase-out ends** | $90,000 single / $180,000 MFJ | $90,000 single / $180,000 MFJ |
| **Best for** | Undergrads, years 1–4 | Grad students, continuing learners |
| **Form required** | Form 8863 | Form 8863 |

*Sources: [IRS Publication 970 (2025)](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p970); [IRS Form 8863 Instructions (2025)](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i8863); [IRS Topic No. 601](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc601)*

**The one rule you cannot bend:** You can only claim *one of these credits per student, per year.* You cannot stack them.

The AOTC wins for most traditional undergrads — higher cap, and up to $1,000 comes back as a refund even if you owe zero tax. For anyone in graduate school or beyond their fourth undergraduate year, the LLC is the right tool.

## Student Loan Interest Deduction: The Above-the-Line Gem

If you paid interest on qualified student loans in 2025, you may be able to **deduct up to $2,500** — even if you take the standard deduction. This is an *above-the-line deduction*, meaning it reduces your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) directly, without itemizing.

### Student Loan Interest Deduction: 2025 Income Limits

| Filing Status | Full Deduction (up to $2,500) | Phase-Out Range | No Deduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single / Head of Household** | MAGI < $85,000 | $85,000 – $100,000 | MAGI ≥ $100,000 |
| **Married Filing Jointly** | MAGI < $170,000 | $170,000 – $200,000 | MAGI ≥ $200,000 |
| **Married Filing Separately** | ❌ Not eligible | — | — |

*Sources: [IRS Publication 970 (2025), Chapter 4](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p970); [IRS Topic No. 456](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc456)*

**What to look for:** Your loan servicer must send **Form 1098-E** if you paid $600 or more in interest during 2025. Paid less than $600? You can still claim the deduction — check your account statements and enter the amount on **Schedule 1 (Form 1040)**.

> **Critical rule:** If someone else claims you as a dependent on their return, you cannot take this deduction — even if you made every payment yourself. Dependency status cascades through multiple benefits. Coordinate before filing.

## Scholarships and Grants: What's Taxable, What Isn't

This one catches students off guard every year. The IRS rule is clear:

- ✅ **Tax-free:** Funds used for tuition, required enrollment fees, required books, and required course supplies
- ⚠️ **Taxable:** Funds used for room and board, travel, optional equipment, or any expense not required for enrollment

### Scholarship Taxability at a Glance

| Use of Scholarship Funds | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| Tuition and mandatory enrollment fees | ✅ Tax-free |
| Required textbooks and course materials | ✅ Tax-free |
| Required lab fees and course supplies | ✅ Tax-free |
| Room and board | ⚠️ Taxable income |
| Personal living expenses | ⚠️ Taxable income |
| Travel to/from school | ⚠️ Taxable income |
| Optional equipment (e.g., laptop not required by school) | ⚠️ Taxable income |

*Source: [IRS Publication 970 (2025), Chapter 1](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p970)*

If your scholarship covers more than tuition and required expenses, the excess must be reported as income. Many students don't realize this until they're mid-filing — and it can produce an unexpected tax bill.

**One advanced tip:** In some situations, strategically *reporting* more scholarship income as taxable can actually *increase* your AOTC credit. This is a legitimate coordination strategy worth a conversation with a tax professional — and the subject of a dedicated post coming soon.

### ⚠️ The "Kiddie Tax" — A Hidden Trap for Students With Large Scholarships or Investment Income

Here's one that blindsides families every year, and it connects directly to taxable scholarships.

If a dependent student has more than **$2,700 in unearned income** in 2025, the amount above $2,700 is taxed at the **parent's marginal rate** — not the student's lower rate. This is the so-called "Kiddie Tax," and it applies to full-time students age **19 through 23** whose earned income does not exceed half of their own support.

**How the three layers work:**

| Unearned Income Layer | Tax Rate Applied |
|---|---|
| First **$1,350** | Tax-free (dependent standard deduction floor) |
| Next **$1,350** (i.e., $1,350–$2,700) | Taxed at the **student's** rate |
| Everything **above $2,700** | Taxed at the **parent's** marginal rate |

**What counts as "unearned income" for this purpose?** Interest, dividends, capital gains — and critically, **taxable scholarship amounts** (room and board portions) and **taxable fellowship grants not reported on a W-2**. Crypto gains, stock dividends, and large housing grants all count.

A student receiving a full scholarship that covers room and board may unknowingly have $10,000+ in taxable unearned income, which gets taxed at the parent's rate if the parent is in a high bracket. This is filed on **Form 8615**, attached to the student's return.

*Sources: [IRS Topic No. 553](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc553); [IRS Form 8615 Instructions (2025)](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/i8615.pdf)*

## New for 2025: No Tax on Tips, No Tax on Overtime

This is one of the most meaningful new benefits for college students who work in service industries. The OBBB introduced two new deductions for tax years **2025 through 2028**, available whether you take the standard deduction or itemize — claimed on **Schedule 1-A (Form 1040)**.

### Tips & Overtime Deductions — Tax Year 2025

| Deduction | Who Qualifies | Maximum | Phase-Out Begins |
|---|---|---|---|
| **No Tax on Tips** | Workers in occupations that *customarily and regularly* receive tips (servers, bartenders, salon workers, delivery drivers, etc.) | **$25,000** | MAGI > $150,000 single / $300,000 MFJ |
| **No Tax on Overtime** | Hourly workers receiving FLSA-required overtime — the "half" portion of time-and-a-half **only** | **$12,500** single / **$25,000** MFJ | MAGI > $150,000 single / $300,000 MFJ |

*Sources: [IRS — What the "No Tax on Tips" Deduction Means for You](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/what-the-no-tax-on-tips-deduction-means-for-you); [IRS Notice 2025-69](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-25-69.pdf)*

**Four things every student worker must know before claiming either deduction:**

1. **You need a valid Social Security Number** (valid for employment) to claim either deduction.
2. **If married, you must file jointly** — married filing separately disqualifies you from both.
3. **Mandatory service charges are not tips.** If a restaurant adds a set 18% service charge to the bill, that portion does not qualify. Only voluntary customer tips count.
4. **Tips must be properly reported.** Unreported tips do not qualify. Report all tips to your employer (Form 4070) and include them on your return.

> ---
> **📋 2025 Overtime Transition Rule — Read This Before You Look at Your W-2**
>
> Because the OBBB passed mid-year, **2025 W-2 forms were not redesigned** to break out tips or overtime separately. Your W-2 total wages box may show a combined figure.
>
> Per **IRS Notice 2025-69**, you can calculate your deductible overtime using pay stubs or employer records. If only a combined overtime total is available, you may generally use **one-third of that total** as your deductible premium — this represents the "extra half" in time-and-a-half pay.
>
> *Example: Your W-2 shows $3,000 in combined overtime pay. You may deduct approximately $1,000 (one-third) as the qualified overtime premium on Schedule 1-A.*
>
> This transition method applies to **2025 only** — W-2s are expected to include separate overtime reporting fields beginning with tax year 2026.
> ---

## Gig Work, NIL Income, and 1099s: The Self-Employment Reality

The tax picture for college students has gotten more complicated in recent years. These categories trip people up the most:

**1. NIL (Name, Image, Likeness) Income**

College athletes receiving NIL compensation — endorsements, social media deals, appearance fees — have fully taxable self-employment income. They'll receive a **1099-NEC** and must file **Schedule C**, paying **self-employment tax (15.3%)** on net earnings on top of regular income tax.

**Important:** The tips and overtime deductions on Schedule 1-A reduce your **regular income tax** only. They do **not** reduce the self-employment tax base (the 15.3% FICA). A student earning $20,000 in NIL income owes self-employment tax on the full net amount — the Schedule 1-A deductions don't touch that bill.

**2. Gig and App-Based Work**

Rideshare driving, freelancing, online reselling — all taxable as self-employment income. Net earnings of **$400 or more** trigger a mandatory filing requirement, full stop. Same rule on SE tax applies: deductions on Schedule 1-A don't reduce the 15.3% self-employment tax.

**3. Payment Apps (Venmo, PayPal, Cash App)**

For tax year 2025, the OBBB restored the original federal 1099-K reporting threshold: a payment platform must send you a 1099-K only if you receive **more than $20,000 AND more than 200 transactions** for goods or services through that platform in the calendar year.

**The rule every student must understand:** The 1099-K threshold only determines whether you *receive a form*. All income from selling goods or providing services is taxable — period — regardless of whether a 1099-K arrives. Personal reimbursements (splitting dinner, collecting rent from a roommate) are not income.

> **State note:** Some states have set their own, lower 1099-K thresholds. Check your state's rules separately.

*Source: [IRS Fact Sheet 2025-08](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/form-1099-k-faqs-general-information)*

## How to File for Free: IRS-Approved Options

There is no reason for a student to pay for tax preparation software. Here are your verified free options for the 2026 filing season:

| Option | Who Qualifies | Start Here |
|---|---|---|
| **IRS Free File — Guided Software** | AGI ≤ **$89,000** for tax year 2025 | [irs.gov/freefile](https://www.irs.gov/filing/free-file-do-your-federal-taxes-for-free) |
| **IRS Free File Fillable Forms** | Any income (self-directed, no step-by-step guidance) | [irs.gov/freefile](https://www.irs.gov/e-file-providers/free-file-fillable-forms) |
| **VITA — Volunteer Income Tax Assistance** | Generally income ≤ **$67,000**, persons with disabilities, limited English proficiency | [irs.gov/vita](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/free-tax-return-preparation-for-qualifying-taxpayers) |
| **MilTax** | Active-duty military and some veterans, any income level | [militaryonesource.mil](https://www.militaryonesource.mil/financial-legal/taxes/miltax-military-tax-services/) |
| **Campus Tax Programs** | Students at participating universities | Check your university financial aid or student services office |

*Source: [IRS Free File (2026 Season)](https://www.irs.gov/filing/free-file-do-your-federal-taxes-for-free)*

**Note:** IRS Direct File was discontinued prior to the 2026 filing season and is no longer available.

Eight IRS-vetted software partners offer full guided federal preparation at no cost for AGI under $89,000. Each sets its own sub-eligibility criteria, but at that income level at least one option will always be available to you.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid Before You Hit Submit

1. **Not coordinating dependency status with parents.** Who claims the student determines who gets the education credits. Have this conversation before *anyone* files.

2. **Forgetting taxable scholarship income.** Room and board covered by a scholarship is taxable — and it can also trigger the Kiddie Tax if total unearned income exceeds $2,700.

3. **Missing the student loan interest deduction.** Independent students paying their own loans qualify for up to $2,500 off AGI — even on the standard deduction.

4. **Not filing because you "didn't make enough."** If any federal tax was withheld from a paycheck, filing is the only way to recover it.

5. **Claiming both AOTC and LLC for the same student in the same year.** You can only claim one. AOTC nearly always wins for undergraduate years 1–4.

6. **Expecting the tips/overtime deduction to reduce self-employment tax.** It doesn't. Schedule 1-A deductions reduce your income tax only. Gig workers and NIL earners still owe 15.3% SE tax on their full net earnings.

7. **Claiming the tips or overtime deduction without meeting all requirements.** You need a valid SSN, must file jointly if married, must have reported your tips, and mandatory service charges don't count.

8. **Assuming no 1099-K means no taxable income.** The form threshold and taxability are completely separate. All income from goods sold or services provided is taxable — always.

9. **Ignoring the Kiddie Tax on large scholarships or investment income.** If a dependent student has more than $2,700 in unearned income (including taxable scholarship portions), the excess is taxed at the parent's rate. Run the numbers before assuming the student's low bracket applies.

## The Roth IRA Move You Probably Haven't Thought About

One final idea that has nothing to do with this year's bill and everything to do with your student's long-term financial future:

**If they have any earned income in 2025, they can contribute to a Roth IRA.**

The 2025 limit is **$7,000** (or total earned income for the year, whichever is less). Contributions are not deductible, but all qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely **tax-free**. For a 20-year-old, that's four-plus decades of compounding with no tax drag at the end.

The deadline to make a 2025 Roth IRA contribution is **April 15, 2026** — the same day taxes are due.

Even $500 invested in a Roth at age 20 becomes something significant by retirement. This is the kind of move that separates financially intentional people from everyone else.

*Source: [IRS Publication 590-A (2025)](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p590a)*

## Your Action Checklist Before April 15, 2026

- [ ] Coordinate dependency status with parents — before anyone files
- [ ] Collect all forms: W-2, 1098-T, 1098-E, 1099s, scholarship letters, and pay stubs
- [ ] Determine AOTC or LLC eligibility — see our [detailed credit guide](https://financepebble.com/tax/education/personal-finance/2026/02/15/education-tax-credits-2025.html)
- [ ] Claim student loan interest deduction if eligible (independent filers; MAGI < $100,000 single)
- [ ] Review scholarship award letter — identify any taxable room-and-board portions
- [ ] Check if Kiddie Tax applies — tally total unearned income (scholarships + investments); if > $2,700, file Form 8615
- [ ] Check tips/overtime deduction eligibility (Schedule 1-A; valid SSN; file jointly if married; MAGI ≤ $150,000)
- [ ] For overtime: use pay stubs and the one-third method if your W-2 doesn't break out amounts separately
- [ ] File free through IRS Free File if AGI ≤ $89,000
- [ ] Make 2025 Roth IRA contribution if student has earned income (deadline: April 15, 2026)
- [ ] Enter bank account info for direct deposit — the IRS strongly encourages electronic refunds for fastest processing
