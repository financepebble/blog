## First, Let's Get on the Same Page

A **529 plan** is a state-sponsored, tax-advantaged account designed for education savings. Contributions grow tax-free and withdrawals are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses like tuition, books, and room and board.

A **Roth IRA** is an individual retirement account funded with after-tax dollars. Contributions grow tax-free, qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, and â€” here's the key point we'll come back to â€” **you can withdraw your contributions (not earnings) at any time, for any reason, penalty-free.**

Both accounts are powerful tools. The question is: when your budget forces you to choose, which one do you fund first?

## The Scenario Most People Don't Think About

## The Hidden Trap: Roth Withdrawals & the FAFSA Prior-Prior Year Rule

## What About the 529's State Tax Deduction?

Fair point â€” one area where the 529 genuinely wins is state tax deductions. Depending on where you live, contributing to your state's 529 plan can reduce your state income tax bill in the year of contribution. Texas has no state income tax, so Texans receive no benefit here. But if you're in New York (deduct up to $5,000 single / $10,000 married), Illinois (up to $10,000 single / $20,000 married), or Virginia (up to $4,000/account with unlimited carryover), the immediate tax savings are real and worth factoring in.

Before assuming the state deduction tips the scales in the 529's favor, verify three things: Does your state actually offer a deduction? Does it require you to use *your own state's* plan (most do)? And what is your actual after-tax dollar savings given your marginal state rate?

For most middle-income families, state tax savings from a 529 won't outweigh the flexibility value of the Roth IRA â€” especially when budgets are already stretched and that flexibility is precisely what you need.

## A Word of Caution: Don't Confuse This With a UTMA/UGMA Account

While we're on education savings vehicles, it's worth flagging a common confusion that catches parents off guard. **UTMA and UGMA accounts are not 529s**, and they carry a risk many families discover far too late.

## The Airplane Oxygen Mask Rule

Here's the financial version of the advice you hear on every flight: *put your own oxygen mask on first.*

You can borrow money for college. There are federal student loans, scholarships, grants, work-study programs, and employer tuition assistance. The financial aid ecosystem, imperfect as it is, exists specifically to help families fund education.

**There is no loan program for retirement.** No one is going to hand your 65-year-old self a check because you sacrificed Roth contributions for your child's sophomore year.

If you retire with insufficient savings, you become a financial burden on your children. The most loving financial decision you can make for your family, long term, is to secure your own future first.
